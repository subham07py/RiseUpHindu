Sanskrit vs. Prakrit

Which Language Is Older?

The debate over whether Sanskrit or Prakrit is the older language has sparked intense discussion among historians, linguists, and scholars. While some modern groups argue that Prakrit predates Sanskrit and that Sanskrit evolved from it, linguistic and historical evidence paints a different picture.

This blog explores the linguistic differences, grammatical structures, and ancient perspectives that point to Sanskrit as the older, foundational language from which Prakrit developed.

Linguistic Comparison: Sanskrit and Prakrit

Alphabet and Phonology

Sanskrit employs a highly refined and comprehensive phonetic system:

  • 14 vowels
  • 33 consonants
  • 1 anusvāra
  • 1 visarga

Total: 49 phonemes

In contrast, Prakrit simplifies this system:

  • About 10 vowels
  • 25–30 consonants (depending on dialect)
  • Less use of anusvāra and visarga

Key Simplifications in Prakrit:

  • Retroflex Consonants: Sanskrit includes ṭa, ṭha, ḍa, ḍha, ṇa — which are often merged or dropped in Prakrit.
  • Aspirated Consonants: Sanskrit aspirates (e.g., kha, gha, jha) are usually replaced with unaspirated equivalents (e.g., ka, ga, ja) in Prakrit.
  • Vocalic Consonants: Sanskrit's ṛ, ḷ, etc., are simplified in Prakrit.
  • Anusvāra and Visarga: Common in Sanskrit, rarely used in Prakrit.
These simplifications clearly indicate that Prakrit is a derivative of Sanskrit, adapted for colloquial and regional use.

Grammar: Complexity vs. Simplicity

Sanskrit is known for its precise grammatical structure, especially as codified in Pāṇini's Ashtadhyayi. Its rules are systematic, logical, and extensive.

On the other hand, Prakrit's grammar is fluid and informal, making it easier for common people but also demonstrating a derivative and simplified nature:

  • Fewer verb conjugations
  • Simplified noun declensions
  • Loss of dual number in many dialects
  • Tendency to reduce compound words

This grammatical evolution supports the theory that Prakrit was born out of the need to simplify Sanskrit for broader use.

Ancient Grammarians on Sanskrit's Primacy

Several respected ancient scholars explicitly stated that Sanskrit is the origin (prakṛti) and Prakrit is the derived form (vikṛti):

  • Vararuchi (Prākṛta Prakāśa) – Sanskrit is the source.
  • Hemachandra (Siddha-Hema-Śabdanuśāsana) – Prakrit comes from Sanskrit.
  • Mārkaṇḍeya, Dhanika, Simhadevaganin, Narasiṃha, Lakṣmīdhara, Vāsudeva – All affirm the same.

Even later grammarians like Nārāyaṇa and Śankara documented the transition from Sanskrit → Prakrit → Apabhraṃśa → modern Indian languages.

The Meaning of "Sanskrit"

The term "Saṃskṛta" literally means:

  • "Sam" – Together, completely
  • "Kṛta" – Done, made, perfected

So Sanskrit = Perfected/Refined language

Whereas Prakrit = Natural/Raw/Original, implying a less structured form.

This distinction in etymology itself points to Sanskrit as the original refined language, from which Prakrit evolved for more casual use.

Responding to Modern Claims

Some modern thinkers argue that because Prakrit was spoken by the masses, it must be older. But this view ignores key facts:

  • Prakrit simplifies Sanskrit's rich phonetic and grammatical base.
  • Sanskrit is seen in Vedic literature, which predates most known Prakrit texts.
  • Sanskrit was used for rituals, philosophy, sciences, while Prakrit was the people's vernacular.
  • No ancient grammarian claimed Prakrit was older.

So while Prakrit reflects the language of everyday life, Sanskrit holds the position of the earlier, more cultivated language.

Conclusion: Sanskrit Came First

  • Phonetic Complexity
  • Grammatical Sophistication
  • Ancient Grammarians' Testimonies
  • Sacred Vedic Use
  • Etymological Meaning
  • Historical Continuity

All of these confirm: Sanskrit is older than Prakrit.

Prakrit didn't precede Sanskrit — it emerged from it, shaped to suit the practical, everyday communication needs of people while maintaining a root connection to the Vedic past.
